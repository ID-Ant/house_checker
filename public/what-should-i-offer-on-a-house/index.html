What Should I Offer on a House in the UK?

Use sold-price evidence, asking price context, and realistic repair costs to work out a more defensible opening offer.

Start with evidence, not a flat percentage

The right offer on a house is not a universal percentage below the asking price. A more reliable approach is to compare the asking price with recent sold-price evidence, review local market conditions, and account for any repairs or improvements the property needs before deciding on a realistic opening number.

For UK buyers, that usually means looking beyond the estate agent's headline figure. If similar homes have sold for less, the property has been listed for a while, or the home clearly needs work, those factors all strengthen the case for offering below asking price.

Why the asking price should not be your only guide

The asking price is a marketing number, not an independent valuation. Some homes are priced high to create negotiation room, while others are priced aggressively to attract multiple offers quickly. That is why buyers who rely only on the asking price often either overpay or make an offer that is easy for the seller to dismiss.

A better method is to compare the asking price with local sold-price evidence and use that to decide whether the property looks fairly priced, overpriced, or potentially competitive.

Check local sold prices

Look for recent sales of similar homes in the same area and property type. The closer the match on location, size, and condition, the more useful that evidence becomes when you decide what to offer.

Compare asking against evidence

If the asking price sits above recent sold prices, you have a stronger case for opening lower. If it sits roughly in line with local evidence, your room to negotiate may be smaller.

Factor in the condition of the property

Visible defects, dated systems, cosmetic work, and likely repairs should change what you are willing to pay. If the property needs decorating, structural work, replacement windows, or a kitchen overhaul, those costs should be reflected in your offer rather than ignored until later.

This is where a structured calculator can help. Use the house offer calculator to compare the asking price with local market context, then adjust the result by adding the work needed at the property.

When it makes sense to offer below asking price

  • The asking price is above recent comparable sold prices.
  • The property has been on the market for longer than expected.
  • The home needs visible repairs or modernisation.
  • Local demand looks softer and sellers have less leverage.
  • You can justify your number with market evidence rather than guesswork.

How to choose a realistic first offer

A realistic first offer should be low enough to reflect the evidence you have gathered, but not so low that it looks unserious. If nearby sold prices support a lower figure and the property needs work, you can explain your position clearly and present the offer as a reasoned number rather than a random discount.

That is usually more persuasive than saying you always offer 10% below asking. Sellers and agents may reject a generic tactic, but they are more likely to engage when you can explain how the asking price compares with local evidence and the cost of work needed.
